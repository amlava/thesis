\chapter{Numerical Experiments}
\label{chap:numerical-experiments}

In this chapter, we will apply \cref{alg:seqprox-sgd} to
two concrete settings. First, in \cref{sec:numerical-example-svm},
we will consider the primal hard-margin support vector machine problem,
which is a popular method in machine learning for classification.
Then, in \cref{sec:numerical-example-inventory-control}, we will
consider the standard control problem of inventory management subject
to uncertainty in demand projections.
The experiments are implemented in PyTorch \cite{paszke2019pytorch}, and the source code
can be found on \href{https://github.com/amlava/thesis}{\textcolor{teal}{github}}.
Code development was partially assisted by OpenAI Codex \cite{openai_codex_2025}.

\section{Classification with support vector machines}
\label{sec:numerical-example-svm}

A common problem in machine learning
is binary classification, where we are given a dataset
$D := \{\, (a_i, y_i) \mid i\in\{\, 1,\dots,N \,\} \,\}$
of size $N\in\N$, where each
$a_i\in\R^n$ is called \textit{feature}
and each $y_i\in \{ -1, 1 \}$ is called
\textit{label}. As a specific example, one can think of the problem of
classifying images (the features) of pets
as either showing a dog (label $1$) or a cat (label $-1$).
The features and labels can be seen as samples from an underlying
probability distribution $\Prob$ on the space $\R^n \times \{-1, 1\}$.
Given this dataset, we aim to learn a rule to classify 
new samples $a_{n+1}\in\R^n$ from the distribution of the features
as either $0$ or $1$. Ideally this rule would
choose the class that maximizes $\Prob(a_{n+1}, \cdot)$.
One approach to find such a rule is called \textit{support vector machine} (SVM),
which is an algorithm for constructing a hyperplane that seperates the two
subsets $\{\, a\in \R^n \mid (a, -1) \in D  \,\}$
and $\{\, a\in \R^n \mid (a, 1) \in D  \,\}$.
New samples are then classified based
on which side of the hyperplane they lie in.
Whether or not such a hyperplane even exists depends on the nature
of the dataset.  Nevertheless, we can always write down the problem of finding
such a hyperplane as a convex optimization problem
with affine constraints:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:svm-hard-margin}
    \tag{SVM}
    \begin{aligned}
    &\min_{x \in \R^n} \,
        \frac{1}{2} |\!|x|\!|^2 \\
    &\textup{s.\,t.}\quad
    y_i \langle a_i, x \rangle \geq 1 \quad\textnormal{for all $i\in\{ \, 1,\dots,N \, \}$}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
If a solution to the above problem exists, we say that the dataset is
\textit{linearly seperable}.
This is often not the case, which is why the above problem is usually relaxed to the unconstrained problem
\[
    \min_{x\in\R^n} \frac{1}{2} |\!|x|\!|^2 + C \sum_{i=1}^{N} \max(0, 1 - y_i \langle a_i, x \rangle), 
\]
where $C\in (0, \infty)$. This problem always has a solution, regardless of whether
or not the dataset is actually linearly seperable. However,
the quality of its solution for the classification task is highly
dependent on the choice of $C$ \cite{hastie2004entire}.

In contrast, our \cref{alg:seqprox-sgd} allows us to bypass this issue because we can directly apply it to
\eqref{eq:svm-hard-margin}.
This problem fits our template problem \eqref{eq:main-problem-copy} by taking
$F_\xi(x)~:=~\frac{1}{2}\norm{x}^2$, $r \equiv 0$,
and
\[
    g(x, (a, y)) := -y \langle a, x \rangle + 1
\]
for $(a, y) \in D$.

To test \cref{alg:seqprox-sgd} on real world data, we use the Wisconsin Diagnostic Breast Cancer dataset \cite{breastcancer}
with $n=30$ features and $N=569$ samples.
Features are standardized, and we use a stratified $70/30$ train/test
split.\footnote{For background information on machine learning evaluation methods,
see the book \cite{geron2022hands}.}
To avoid perfect separability, we flip labels independently with probability $0.1$.
As the penalty function sequence $(h_k)_{k\in\N_0}$, we use the Huber-type penalty from \cref{ex:huber-type-penalty}.
The objective is $1$-strongly convex, so we use the parameter schedule suggested by \cref{cor:convergence-rates-in-expectation}:
\[
    \eta_0 = 2,\qquad
    \eta_k = \frac{2}{k},\qquad
    \gamma_k = \ln(k+1),\qquad
    \delta_0 = 1,\qquad
    \delta_k = \frac{1}{k}, \qquad
    w_k = \eta_k^{-1},
\]
for $k\in\N$.

Since there does not exist a solution to this problem, we will track the
misclassification rates\footnote{The misclassification rate is defined as the percentage of falsely classified examples in a given set.}
of the classifiers induced by the sequence of iterates $(\bar{x}_k)_{n\in\N}$, generated by \cref{alg:seqprox-sgd}, evaluated
on the training and test sets, respectively.
We repeat the experiment over $10$ independent runs
and report the mean and standard
deviation of the train and test misclassification rates over the sequence of averaged iterates $(\bar{x}_k)_{k\in\N}$.
\Cref{fig:svm-breast-cancer-errors} shows the average training
and test error across iterations with $\pm 1$ standard deviation bands.
After $400$ iterations, the final training error is
$0.1357 \pm 0.0158$ and the final test error is
$0.1386 \pm 0.0192$.

    \label{fig:svm-breast-cancer-errors}
\end{figure}

\section{Inventory control}
\label{sec:numerical-example-inventory-control}
 
This example is adapted from Section~4.8.2 in \cite{evans1983introduction}.
We consider a discrete-time inventory problem over a finite horizon
$\{0,\dots,n-1\}$, where $n\in\N$.
Let $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ be a probability space
and let $\zeta\colon\Omega\to\R^p$ be a random variable.
For each period $k\in\{\, 0,\dots,n-1 \,\}$,
we introduce the following quantities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[-] $y_k(\zeta)$: inventory level at the beginning of period $k$, subject to uncertainty,
    \item[-] $x_k$: order quantity placed in period $k$,
    \item[-] $d_k(\zeta)$: customer demand in period $k$, subject to uncertainty,
    \item[-] $\gamma$: per-unit ordering cost,
    \item[-] $\beta$: per-unit holding cost.
\end{itemize}
The inventory evolves according to
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sec:numerical-example-inventory-control:dynamics}
    y_{k+1}(\zeta) = y_k(\zeta) + x_k - d_k(\zeta)
\end{equation}
for $k \in \{0,\dots,n-1\}$,
with a prescribed deterministic initial inventory $y_0 \in (0,\infty)$.
We seek to minimize the expected total ordering and holding cost subject
to almost-sure nonnegativity of the inventory at every period.
Adding a quadratic regulariser $\lambda\|x\|^2$ with $\lambda\in(0,\infty)$
for stability, and imposing a finite ordering capacity
$x_{\max}\in(0, \infty)$, the problem reads
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:inventory-control-example}
    \begin{aligned}
        &\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^{n}} \;
            \mathbb{E}\Bigl(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \gamma x_k
            + \beta \sum_{k=1}^{n} y_k(\zeta)\Bigr)
            + \lambda\|x\|^2 \\[6pt]
        &\text{s.\,t.}\quad
            y_{k+1}(\zeta) = y_k(\zeta) + x_k - d_k(\zeta)
            \quad\text{a.\,s. for all}
            \; k\in\{0,\dots,n-1\} \\[4pt]
        &\hspace{1.3cm}
            y_k(\zeta) \geq 0
            \quad\text{a.\,s. for all} \; k\in\{1,\dots,n\} \\[4pt]
        &\hspace{1.3cm}
            0 \leq x_k \leq x_{\max}
            \quad \text{for all} \; k \in \{\, 0,\dots,n-1 \,\}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
We can eliminate the state variables $(y_k)_{k\in\{\, 0,\dots,n-1 \,\}}$ by
recursively solving the dynamics \eqref{eq:sec:numerical-example-inventory-control:dynamics}.
This yields
\[
    y_k(\zeta) = y_0 + \sum_{j=0}^{k-1}\bigl(x_j - d_j(\zeta)\bigr),
\]
for all $k\in\{\, 0,\dots,n-1 \,\}$.
The nonnegativity constraints thus become linear inequalities in $x$.
To write them compactly, let
$A = (a_{ij})_{i,j\in\{1,\dots,n\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$
be the lower-triangular matrix with
\[
    a_{ij} := \begin{cases}
        1, \quad\text{if $i \geq j$}, \\
        0, \quad\text{else.}
    \end{cases}
\]
We further define
\[
    d(\zeta) := \bigl(d_0(\zeta),\dots,d_{n-1}(\zeta)\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^n,
    \qquad
    \mathbf{1} := (1,\dots,1)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^n.
\]
Setting
\[
    v := \mathbf{1}^\top(\beta A + \gamma I_n),
    \qquad
    \eta(\zeta) := \beta\,\mathbf{1}^\top A\, d(\zeta),
\]
and
\[
    r(x) := \begin{cases}
        0, \quad\text{if $x \in [0, x_{\max}]^n$}, \\
        \infty, \quad\text{else,}
    \end{cases}
\]
problem~\eqref{eq:inventory-control-example} fits our template~\eqref{eq:main-problem} by
taking the proximal term $r$ as above,
\begin{align*}
    F(x,(i, \zeta)) &:= \langle v, x \rangle - \eta(\zeta) + \lambda \norm{x}^2,  \quad\text{and} \\
    g(x, (i, \zeta)) &:= -A_i x + A_i d(\zeta) - y_0,
\end{align*}
where we let $A_i\in\R^{1\times n}$ denote the $i$-th row of $A$ for $i\in\{\, 0,\dots,39 \,\}$.
For data generation, we use a horizon of $n = 40$
periods and generate $2000$ demand scenarios.
That is, we generate $2000$ trajectories $\{\, d_0(\zeta),\dots,d_{39}(\zeta) \,\}$.
The samples are generated independently, for each period $k \in \{\, 0,\dots,39 \,\}$,
according to a lognormal distribution
\[
    d_k \sim \operatorname{Lognormal}\bigl(\log(m_k),\,0.2^2\bigr),
    \qquad
    m_k := 1.5 + 0.30 \sin\Bigl(\frac{2\pi k}{12}\Bigr).
\]
The demand follows a seasonal pattern with noise.
For interpretability of the parameter $\lambda$, we
rescale all inventory quantities by the empirical mean demand
\[
    s := \frac{1}{2000\cdot 40}\sum_{i=1}^{2000}\sum_{k=0}^{39} d_k^{(i)},
\]
where $d_k^{(i)}$ denotes the $i$-th demand scenario in period $k\in\{\, 0,\dots,39 \,\}$
for $i\in \{\, 1,\dots, 2000 \,\}$.
The parameters are chosen as
\[
    \gamma = 0.6,
    \qquad
    \beta  = 0.35,
    \qquad
    \lambda = 0.5,
    \qquad
    y_0 = \frac{8}{s},
    \qquad
    x_{\max} = \frac{4}{s}.
\]
Since the empirical objective is $2\lambda$-strongly convex,
we apply \cref{alg:seqprox-sgd} with the strongly convex schedule
from \cref{cor:convergence-rates-in-expectation}:
\[
    \eta_0 = \frac{1}{\lambda},
    \quad
    \eta_k = \frac{1}{\lambda k},
    \quad
    \gamma_k  = c_\gamma \ln(k+2),
    \quad
    \delta_k  = \frac{c_\delta}{k+1},
    \quad
    w_k = \eta_k^{-1},
\]
where $k \in \mathbb{N}_0, c_\gamma, c_\delta\in(0,\infty)$,
and the penalty function sequence
$(h_k)_{k\in\N_0}$ is again
the Huber-type penalty sequence from \cref{ex:huber-type-penalty}.
The parameters $c_\gamma$ and $c_\delta$
are tuned by minimizing
the relative distance to the true optimum after $1000$ iterations\footnote{Of course,
the distance to the ground-truth minimizer is not known in
practice. It is not clear how to tune the parameters in the general case
of problem \eqref{eq:main-problem-copy}.
If $r\equiv 0$, however, one could instead consider the norm of the gradient
of the penalized objective, $\norm{ \nabla f(\bar{x}_k) + \gamma_k \nabla \pi(\bar{x}_k)}$,
as a scoring criterion. If $\bar{x}_k$ is close
to $x^\star$, this value is close to zero. What constitutes a good scoring criterion
in the general case remains an open question.}
over all combinations of
\[
    c_\gamma \in \{\, 160, 170, 180, 190, 200 \,\}
\]
and
\[
    c_\delta \in \{\, 0.05, 0.075, 0.1, 0.125, 0.15 \,\}.
\]
The tuned pair is $(c_\gamma, c_\delta) = (180, 0.125)$,
which is then used to rerun the algorithm for $10000$ iterations.
\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../code/examples/optimal_control/inventory/sipm_inventory_gap.pdf}
\caption{Inventory control: relative distance $\|\bar x_k-x^\star\|/\|x^\star\|$
over iterations. The shaded region shows $\pm 3$ standard deviations,
and the dotted horizontal line indicates the level $0.2$.}
\label{fig:inventory-gap}
\end{figure}
 
To test the performance of our algorithm, we determine the ground-truth solution
with CVX \cite{diamond2016cvxpy}. Note that strong convexity of the objective,
along with the fact that we sample a finite amount of scenarios for the affine
constraints,
guarantee the existence of a unique minimizer
$x^\star$ (see \cref{prop:strongly-convex-implies-lower-level-sets}).
We repeat the experiment over $10$ independent runs for 10000 iterations each
on the full dataset,
and report the mean and standard deviation of the averaged iterates
$(\bar x_k)_{k\in\mathbb{N}}$.
\Cref{fig:inventory-gap} shows the relative distance
$\|\bar x_k-x^\star\| / \|x^\star\|$,
where $x^\star$ denotes the unique CVX minimizer,
together with $\pm 3$ standard deviation bands and a dotted reference
line at level $0.2$.
After $10000$ iterations, the final relative distance is
$1.74\times10^{-1} \pm 1.2\times10^{-3}$.
